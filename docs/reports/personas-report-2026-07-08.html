What the research said (and how it's applied)

Two parallel research agents: one on shipped games (Heat Signature, Nemesis, RimWorld, Watch Dogs Legion, Dwarf Fortress), one on the academic/professional literature (Talk of the Town, Prom Week, the Oz Project, Grinblat's Caves of Qud talks, Kate Compton, Emily Short). Full digests in the wiki under the new #domain/procgen tag. The five findings that shaped the build:

  1. Players attach to inferences, not data. Few, specific, mutually consistent facts with deliberate gaps beat rich profiles. Watch Dogs Legion had the deepest database and the flattest characters — its dialogue wasn't conditioned on the data. So: every sentence on our sheets is generated from the persona's life cluster; a night nurse and a payroll clerk cannot draw the same line.
  2. One unresolved hook, ending the sheet (Heat Signature). Hooks name a relationship and a want, and never resolve. Template authoring rules: concrete nouns, no emotion words, no "because".
  3. Asymmetric edges (Nemesis, by way of no-combat). ~30% of adults get a one-sided line about a specific neighbor, coworker, or someone on their street — clickable, and the other person's sheet doesn't know. The player who clicks both is the only one who sees the whole story.
  4. Shared lore nouns (Dwarf Fortress legends). Each city generates ~15 named entities — the blackout of '18, The Okafor Radio, the diner that used to be the bank — that many sheets touch. Two strangers mentioning the same bakery is the cheapest "this world existed before you looked."
  5. Curation over dumping (James Ryan's story sifting). Each building's occupants list leads with its most interesting pattern: "Two residents here are seeing each other. The floors between them don't know yet."

The apophenia engine — the thesis, in one place

Apophenia is the human tendency to perceive meaningful patterns and intent where none exists. The term entered procgen design mainly via Tynan Sylvester (RimWorld), from "The Simulation Dream": "This apophenia — this perception of personality and intent where there is none — is the key to making a simulation game work… we only need to show the simulation equivalent of moving balls and let the player layer in their own emotional perceptions." The film-world cousin is the Kuleshov effect: the same neutral face reads as hunger or grief depending on the shot it's cut against — meaning comes from juxtaposition, supplied by the viewer.

The overnight research converged on one finding: players don't attach to generated data, they attach to the inferences the data forces them to make. Watch Dogs Legion had the richest NPC database and the flattest characters; Heat Signature's characters are a name plus one sentence and people remember them. So this system doesn't generate stories — it generates evidence, engineered so your mind builds the story:

  • Template authoring rules: concrete nouns only, no emotion words, no "because", never resolve. "The second toothbrush is still in the cup" — the system never says lonely; you do.
  • The hook always ends the sheet, unresolved — the last thing you read is an open question.
  • Domain words (Jason Grinblat, Caves of Qud): one noun-world per persona threaded through detail, hook, and epithet, so independently drawn lines read as one coherent obsession.
  • One-sided relations: A's sheet mentions B; B's sheet doesn't know. Only the player who clicks both witnesses the "story", and neither resident is aware they're in it.
  • Shared lore nouns: ~15 per city (the blackout of '18, the 4 a.m. ferry) recur across strangers' sheets, so coincidences feel like one pre-existing world.

Recall Owen T. Taylor: night-shift security guard, hook "Walks past the 4 a.m. ferry on the way home. It is not on the way home." His shift, the ferry (a city lore noun), and the detour were drawn by separate template rolls — no code connected them. Your brain instantly manufactures motive: what's at the ferry? Who is he hoping to see? That involuntary inference is the engine working; the system's job is to reliably produce material with exactly that kind of gap in it, then stop one sentence early.

Guardrails held

  • Determinism contract: all new streams are namespaced (::personas::*, ::names::*); no Math.random()/Date.now(); ages measured against a fixed in-world date. cityGolden still passes byte-identical.
  • Derived, never stored: the whole directory recomputes from seed, module-cached with the shared cache-key recipe. Zustand holds one new runtime field.
  • The stereotype trap (flagged by the name-bias literature): ethnicity influences surname only, softly; profession weights key off district character only. Name pools and a building's income band never condition each other.
  • Quality review caught and fixed: bellhops at law firms (employment affinity ladder), kids whose tenure contradicted their parents' (household move-history inheritance), a married couple sharing the same 3 a.m. line (per-household dedupe), two buildings at the same address (collision bump).

#89 v3 — helicopter tours as real splines

The v2 model bowed each hop with a quadratic bezier but sampled it at only 4 chords and met every pad at a hard corner. v3 threads one closed centripetal Catmull-Rom spline through the whole tour (stops + the seeded bow midpoints as control points), samples 10 sub-legs per hop, and adds a seeded climb-out/descend altitude bump per hop — so curvature is continuous through every stop and cross-town legs get real air under them. The leg schema and shader are untouched; it's just more, shorter transit legs. Verified: deterministic, legs chain continuously around the closed loop, time windows tile 0..1 exactly, typecheck/lint/golden clean.

Round 3 — the columns, the schools, the connections

Morning direction, delivered: the three detail panels became one progressive-disclosure system — Miller columns, like the macOS Finder. Click a building and the drill starts: district → building → its occupants split into companies and residents → a company's staff → an employee's sheet → the person their story points at. Every hop is one column to the right; Back/Forward walk the trail without losing it.

  • Three views, cycled from the header: flat side-by-side columns with aligned headers (top-left dock), the CSS-3D deck where earlier columns recede as tilted slivers you click to jump back, and collapsed — just the top card with "· N behind".
  • Streets are entities now: click a road in inspect mode and its column lists every building, company, and resident on it, with the street lit up x-ray in the scene.
  • Connection arcs: selecting a person draws their commute arc (thick, mode-coloured) plus thin violet arcs to the homes of their partner, family, and the neighbor their story mentions — the social graph, readable on the skyline.
  • Schools: 71 real ones at the default seed (46 elementary — one per home district, 16 middle, 9 high), every kid enrolled at the nearest school of their age tier. 90% walk; the rest ride a school-bus yellow arc. Teachers hire in through the normal employment ladder, and a school's column lists staff and students.
  • Badge hovers: the astrology and MBTI badges now explain themselves — hover Taurus and get "Immovable once settled; keeps the good chair and the grudge."

Retired in the process: BuildingInfoPanel and PersonaPanel (fully ported into the column bodies). Round 4 — interconnection, follow mode, and the writing lab
